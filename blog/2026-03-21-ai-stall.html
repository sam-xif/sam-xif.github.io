The 2028 Global Intelligence Shortage

March 21, 2026

I work in software, and since the beginning of this year, 2026, I have been investing in integrating AI into my workflow. AI has gotten strikingly good at coding, to the point where it writes close to 90% of my code. Many of my coworkers at Stripe are reporting a similar experience. Claude's latest Opus model is a significant step above previous frontier iterations, and engineers across the company have been using it extensively. I am sure this is playing out across the whole industry as well.

The news isn't all great though. A step function in capability means a step function in cost. Recently, I caught wind of news that a large software company, typically considered best in class, is pumping the brakes on AI spend. The team responsible for rolling out AI tools must have received a directive to start constraining costs because the company's bottom line was starting to feel it, and they forced all engineers to use a lower tier model unknowingly. Several engineers at the company grumbled about how the lower model tier's output was noticably and significantly worse, and it led to wasted cycles spent on reviewing and correcting the worse model's output.

This got me to thinking about whether the optimistic AI adoption scenarios painted in pieces like the Citrini report and Matt Schumer's now infamous Something Big Is Happening post.

My take is that the AI replacement doom and gloom might be overstated, and I'll do my best here to explain why.

Running headlong toward the cost wall

As I just mentioned, it seems that there are stirrings of companies starting to feel the burn of their cash—er—burn, and that different tiers of models have different tiers of capacity to automate work.

Models with larger parameter counts and larger context windows have been shown to perform significantly better on tasks of longer horizon and complexity, but of course, you get what you pay for. The era of venture capital subsidies of below-market pricing among frontier giants such as Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google will have to come to an end at some point when the market returns to rationality, just as Uber investors subsidized the millenial lifestyle for a time before fares skyrocketed.

And when that time comes, I contend that, at least in the short-to-medium term, access to frontier reasoning capabilities will be constrained to the hands of the rich and powerful few, much like other forms of wealth in our top-heavy capitalist society. I find this vision of the future to be the most coherent, and frankly the most boring...which probably means that it is likely. The possible lack of future access to frontier model updates for the long tail of unmagnificent enterprises, coupled with recent findings about the nature of how the current generation of AI models paradoxically may decrease productivity, or at least make work more cognitively exhausting, leads me to conclude that an economy-scale disruption in the vein of the Citrini report is highly unlikely, and the status quo will probably remain firmly in place for quite some time.

I will attempt to reinvigorate in the reader a revolutionary spirit toward the end of this post with some thoughts about what actions we can take to try to generate escape velocity from this boring future. But to start, here are the main pillars of my argument:

  1. AI buildout bottleneck: The cost of the AI build out is high and physical bottlenecks are becoming manifest. Recent geopolitical instability and its effects on energy prices are also threatening to further slow progress

  2. AI Adoption and productivity gains may not be sustainable: Investment in AI tools is reportedly not generating ROI for many enterprises. AI is making workers feel more productive, but with the consequence of increased cognitive load and burnout. The oft-cited METR task horizon benchmark is not very reflective of reality.

  3. This may be the outcome the economy wants: People are scared of disruption. The picture painted in the Citrini report is a bleak one, especially given that governments typically lag significantly behind the private sector in response to such events. Frankly, I also have little faith that the current governmental status quo (in the US, at least) is well-equipped for the creative policymaking that such an economic disruption would call for.

AI buildout economics

If you're tapped into the sphere of popular tech podcasts, such as the recently started Cheeky Pint podcast started by cofounder of Stripe John Collison, you may have heard several guests, such as Elon Musk talking about a severe shortage of turbines that are used to convert natural gas-powered motion into electricity. Gas-fired turbine lead times have soared to as much as seven years. That means that if you aren't already in the queue, you're waiting close to a decade to getting just the turbines, which are necessary but not sufficient for end-to-end power generation from natural gas.

Forecasts for data center energy demand vary wildly though. According to a report from the World Resources Institute, forecasts for US data center yearly power demand in 2030 range from 200 terawatt-hours per year (TWh/yr) to 1,050 TWh/yr. Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory seems to have a reasonable estimate somewhere in between those: 325T TWh/yr to 580 TWh/yr, so I'll accept the midpoint, 453 TWh/yr, as a provisional estimate of yearly power demand for data centers in the US in 2030.

Frontier model providers themselves seem to be having trouble estimating demand for their own services. Project Stargate, announced right at the start of President Trump's second term in January 2025, was supposed to be a $500 billion initiative to build out AI-focused data centers by 2029, but negotiations between OpenAI and Oracle to expand Oracle's data center in Abilene, Texas recently fell through due in part to OpenAI's shifting capacity forecasts.
